== Foreword

Most architecture reviews happen too late.

They happen after an incident. After a failed scaling event. After a key
engineer leaves and the team realises nobody else understands how the system
actually works.

The good news is that a review conducted honestly — even late — is worth
more than one conducted early and forgotten. The bad news is that the longer
you wait, the more uncomfortable the findings tend to be.

This guide describes how I run an architecture review. It's not a framework
or a methodology — it's a practical sequence that has consistently surfaced
the problems that matter in systems I've been brought in to assess.

== When to Review

An architecture review is most valuable at these moments:

* *After significant growth* — in traffic, team size, or feature surface area
* *Before major investment* — new platform, new market, new capability
* *After a serious incident* — particularly one that surprised the team
* *During leadership change* — a new CTO, Head of Engineering, or architect
* *When velocity declines* — and nobody can clearly explain why
* *When the system feels fragile* — even if the metrics don't show it yet

The trigger is almost never "the architecture is broken". It's usually a
quieter signal: things that used to be easy have become hard, and the team
can't articulate why.

== Principles

=== Outcomes, Not Opinions

A review should produce findings, not preferences. "You should use Kubernetes"
is an opinion. "Your deployment pipeline requires manual coordination across
three teams, adding an average of two days to each release" is a finding.

Findings describe what is. Recommendations describe what could be different
and why the change would matter. Keep them separate.

=== Respect the History

Every decision that looks questionable now was made by someone under constraints
you may not fully understand. The system exists, it works (mostly), and it got
the business to where it is today. A review that starts from "this is all wrong"
will lose the trust of the team before it produces any value.

Seek to understand the constraints before you judge the decisions.

=== Talk to the Team, Not Just the Code

The codebase will show you what the system does. The team will tell you why.
Architecture is as much about the decisions that were considered and rejected
as it is about the ones that were made. Only people can tell you that story.

=== Written Over Verbal

A review that lives in someone's head or in a slide deck that nobody opens
again is a review that never happened. Write it down. Make it specific.
Make it findable.

== The Review Process

=== Phase 1: Scoping

Before looking at any code or infrastructure, define what you're reviewing and why.

==== Questions to answer:

* What triggered this review?
* What are the business and technical goals for the next 12-18 months?
* What areas of the system are causing the most concern?
* What's explicitly out of scope?

==== Outputs:

* A one-page scope document listing the systems, qualities, and concerns under review
* Agreement on who the findings will be shared with

_Without a clear scope, a review expands to fill all available time and
produces a report that's comprehensive but not actionable._

---

=== Phase 2: Context Gathering

Gather existing documentation, architecture diagrams, and operational data.
Don't expect it to be complete or current — the gaps are themselves a finding.

==== Collect:

* Existing architecture diagrams (and note their age)
* Deployment pipeline configuration
* Incident history (last 6-12 months)
* Monitoring dashboards and alerting configuration
* On-call runbooks
* Any existing ADRs (Architecture Decision Records)

==== Ask:

* What documentation exists and how current is it?
* Who drew the last architecture diagram, and when?
* Where do new engineers go to understand the system?

_If the answer to "where's the architecture documentation?" is a person's
name, that's a finding._

---

=== Phase 3: Technical Assessment

This is the core of the review. Work through each area systematically.

==== Structure & Boundaries

* How is the system decomposed? Along what lines?
* Are the boundaries between components well-defined?
* Where is coupling tightest?
* What would break if you removed a single component?

==== Data

* Where does data live? How does it move?
* What are the consistency guarantees? Are they understood by the team?
* Where is the authoritative source of truth for key domain concepts?
* Is data being duplicated across services, and if so, is that deliberate?

==== Deployment & Operations

* How does code get from a developer's machine to production?
* How long does a deployment take? How often does it happen?
* What manual steps exist in the deployment process?
* What does the on-call experience look like?

==== Resilience & Failure Modes

* What are the known single points of failure?
* What happens when a downstream dependency is unavailable?
* When was failover last tested?
* What's the team's confidence in their disaster recovery process?

==== Security & Compliance

* How are secrets managed?
* What's the permission model? When was it last reviewed?
* Are there compliance requirements that influence architectural decisions?
* Where does personal data live, and who can access it?

==== Observability

* Can you trace a request from ingress to final response?
* Are alerts tied to meaningful thresholds, or have they been silenced?
* How long does it take to diagnose a cross-service issue?

---

=== Phase 4: Team Conversations

Schedule conversations with engineers, tech leads, and product people.
The goal is to understand the human system around the technical one.

==== For engineers:

* What's the most frustrating part of working on this system?
* What would you fix if you had a free week?
* What part of the system are you most nervous about?
* What do you wish you'd known when you joined?

==== For tech leads:

* Where do you spend most of your time — building or firefighting?
* What decisions would you make differently if you could go back?
* What's the biggest risk the team is carrying right now?

==== For product:

* What capabilities does the business need in the next 12 months?
* Where does the system slow down the business?
* What's the cost of the current architecture in terms they can feel?

_The most important finding in any review often comes from the team, not
the code. Listen for the thing that everyone knows but nobody has said
out loud._

---

=== Phase 5: Findings & Recommendations

==== Structure your findings as:

. *Observation* — what you found, stated as fact
. *Impact* — what effect this has on delivery, reliability, cost, or risk
. *Recommendation* — what to do about it
. *Priority* — how urgently it needs attention

==== Prioritise using:

* *Critical* — actively causing incidents, data loss, or security exposure
* *High* — significantly impacting velocity, reliability, or cost
* *Medium* — creating friction that will worsen as the system grows
* *Low* — worth addressing but not urgent

==== Keep it honest:

* Don't soften findings to avoid discomfort
* Don't recommend changes you can't justify with evidence
* Acknowledge what's working well — not everything will be a problem
* Be specific enough that someone could act on each recommendation
without asking you what you meant

---

=== Phase 6: Handover

A review is only as good as the action it produces.

* Present findings to the team first — before leadership
* Give people a chance to correct factual errors or add context
* Produce a written report that can stand on its own
* Propose a follow-up checkpoint (30, 60, or 90 days) to review progress

_The worst outcome of a review is a report that everyone agrees with
and nobody acts on. Tie each recommendation to a named owner and a
time horizon._

== Common Anti-Patterns

=== The Reassurance Review

A review commissioned to confirm that everything is fine. The scope is narrow,
the questions are leading, and the findings are mild. This happens when
leadership wants validation, not insight.

_If you've been asked to do a review and told the expected outcome in advance,
you're not being asked to review — you're being asked to endorse._

=== The Boil-the-Ocean Review

A review that tries to assess everything and takes so long that the system
has changed by the time the report lands. Scope aggressively. A focused
review that surfaces three actionable findings is worth more than a
comprehensive one that surfaces thirty.

=== The Technology Review Disguised as Architecture

A review that focuses on technology choices (should we use Kafka? should we
move to Kubernetes?) rather than architectural qualities (is the system
resilient? can we deploy independently? is coupling controlled?).

_Technology choices are interesting. Architectural properties are what matter.
You can have excellent technology choices and terrible architecture — and
vice versa._

=== The Blame Audit

A review that becomes an exercise in finding who made bad decisions. This
destroys trust and ensures the next review will never happen. Focus on the
decisions and their consequences, not the people who made them.

== Closing Thoughts

An architecture review is not an exam. It's not a judgement on the team or
the system. It's a structured opportunity to step back from the day-to-day
and see the whole picture — the picture that's invisible when you're focused
on your part of the system.

The teams that benefit most from reviews are the ones who didn't need to be
forced into having one. They asked for it because they wanted to see clearly —
even if what they saw was uncomfortable.

That willingness to look honestly at your own system is the single best
predictor of engineering quality I've encountered in 30 years.
