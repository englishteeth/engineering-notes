== Foreword

Every codebase has technical debt. That's not the problem.

The problem is that most teams treat technical debt as a single, amorphous backlog
item labelled "tech debt" that competes with feature work and always loses.

Debt isn't one thing. It has different causes, different costs, and different urgencies.
Some of it is killing you slowly. Some of it doesn't matter. Some of it is load-bearing
and should be left alone.

This checklist is a triage tool. It won't eliminate your debt, but it will help you
see it clearly, classify it honestly, and decide where to spend your limited time
for maximum effect.

== Section 1: Visibility & Awareness

=== Do You Know What You Owe?

* [ ] Can your team list the top five pieces of technical debt in the system?
* [ ] Would two different team members produce the same list?
* [ ] Is technical debt tracked alongside feature work, or kept in a separate
backlog that nobody reviews?

_If the team can't agree on what the debt is, you can't prioritise it.
Invisible debt is the most expensive kind — not because it's worse,
but because it compounds without scrutiny._

---

=== Debt Archaeology

* [ ] Do you know _why_ each significant piece of debt exists?
* [ ] Was it a deliberate shortcut taken under time pressure, or an accident
that was never corrected?
* [ ] Is the original context still valid?

_Deliberate debt taken with open eyes is a legitimate engineering decision.
Accidental debt that nobody noticed is a quality problem. They need different
responses. The first needs a repayment plan. The second needs a process change._

---

=== The Debt Register

* [ ] Do you maintain a lightweight register of known technical debt?
* [ ] Does each entry describe the impact, not just the symptom?
* [ ] Is the register reviewed regularly, or is it where debt goes to die?

_A debt register that's updated once and never reviewed is worse than no register
at all — it creates the illusion of management without the practice of it._

== Section 2: Classification

Not all debt is equal. These categories help distinguish between debt that demands
attention and debt that can wait.

=== Reckless vs Prudent

* [ ] Was this debt incurred knowingly, with a plan to address it?
* [ ] Or was it incurred through ignorance, haste, or neglect?

_Prudent debt is a tool. Reckless debt is a warning sign. If most of your debt
is reckless, you have a process problem, not a backlog problem._

---

=== Load-Bearing Debt

* [ ] Are there pieces of debt that the system now depends on?
* [ ] Would removing this debt require significant rearchitecting?
* [ ] Is there code that's ugly but stable, widely depended upon, and rarely changed?

_Not all debt should be repaid. Some of it has become structural. Rewriting a
stable, load-bearing component for aesthetic reasons is not debt repayment —
it's risk introduction._

---

=== Compounding Debt

* [ ] Which pieces of debt make other work slower or more error-prone?
* [ ] Are there areas of the codebase that every developer avoids or dreads?
* [ ] Do you have components where the cost of every change includes the cost
of working around existing problems?

_This is the debt that matters most. Compounding debt taxes every future change.
Left alone, it turns a productive team into a slow one — not through any single
decision, but through the accumulated friction of a hundred workarounds._

---

=== Dormant Debt

* [ ] Do you have debt in parts of the system that are rarely changed?
* [ ] Is anyone proposing to fix debt in stable, low-traffic code?
* [ ] Would the effort to fix it produce any measurable improvement?

_Debt in code that nobody touches and that performs adequately is debt you can
live with. Prioritising it over compounding debt in high-traffic code is a
misallocation of engineering time._

== Section 3: Impact Assessment

=== Velocity Impact

* [ ] Has your team's delivery speed measurably declined over the past six months?
* [ ] Can you attribute that decline to specific areas of the codebase?
* [ ] Are estimates for "simple" changes growing because the surrounding code
is difficult to work with?

_Velocity decline is the most visible symptom of compounding debt. But it's also
the easiest to misattribute. Make sure you're measuring the right cause._

---

=== Incident Correlation

* [ ] Are your production incidents concentrated in specific areas of the codebase?
* [ ] Do the same components appear repeatedly in post-mortem timelines?
* [ ] Have you mapped your incident history against your known debt?

_If the same component appears in three post-mortems in six months, that's not
bad luck — it's a pattern. Incident data is the most persuasive argument for
debt repayment you will ever have._

---

=== Onboarding Cost

* [ ] How long does it take a new developer to become productive?
* [ ] Are there areas of the system that require oral tradition to understand?
* [ ] Do new joiners routinely ask "why is it done this way?" and receive the
answer "historical reasons"?

_If onboarding requires tribal knowledge, the debt is not just in the code —
it's in the organisational dependency on specific individuals. That's a bus
factor problem disguised as a codebase problem._

---

=== Recruitment & Retention Signal

* [ ] Are developers citing codebase quality as a reason for frustration or departure?
* [ ] Is the state of the codebase affecting your ability to attract talent?
* [ ] Do candidates ask about technical debt in interviews?

_Engineers talk. The state of your codebase is not a secret. If it's driving
people away, the cost of the debt includes the cost of the people you've lost
and the people you can't hire._

== Section 4: Prioritisation

=== The Two-Axis Test

For each significant piece of debt, ask:

* [ ] How often is this code changed? (High frequency = higher priority)
* [ ] How painful is it when it is changed? (High pain = higher priority)

_Debt in frequently-changed, high-pain code is your top priority. Debt in
rarely-changed, low-pain code is your lowest. This is a better prioritisation
framework than severity alone._

---

=== Quick Wins

* [ ] Are there small, low-risk improvements that would immediately reduce friction?
* [ ] Can any debt be addressed incrementally as part of feature work?
* [ ] Is your team allowed to improve code they touch, or are they pressured to
make the minimum change?

_The Boy Scout Rule — leave the code better than you found it — is the most
effective debt repayment strategy that exists. But it only works if the culture
supports it. If developers are measured on story points and punished for
"scope creep", the debt will grow._

---

=== The Business Case

* [ ] Can you quantify the cost of your top debt items in terms the business understands?
  (Slower delivery, more incidents, higher onboarding time, recruitment difficulty)
* [ ] Have you presented debt repayment as an investment rather than a cost?
* [ ] Does your leadership understand the difference between "we should fix this"
and "this is costing us X per quarter"?

_"We need to refactor" is a request. "This component has caused four incidents in
three months, each costing an average of 12 hours of engineering time" is a
business case. One gets deferred. The other gets funded._

== Section 5: Repayment Strategy

=== Dedicated Time vs Continuous Improvement

* [ ] Do you allocate regular time for debt repayment, or rely on goodwill and slack?
* [ ] If you have a "20% time" or "tech debt sprint", does it actually happen?
* [ ] Is there accountability for debt repayment, or does it quietly slip when
deadlines tighten?

_Allocated time that consistently gets cancelled is worse than no allocation.
It signals that the organisation values debt repayment in theory but not in
practice. Engineers stop believing in it — and stop proposing it._

---

=== Strangler Pattern Thinking

* [ ] For large-scale debt, have you considered incremental replacement over
big-bang rewrites?
* [ ] Can you put a new implementation alongside the old one and migrate gradually?
* [ ] Is there a component the team wants to rewrite from scratch — and have you
questioned whether that's really necessary?

_The urge to rewrite is strong and almost always underestimates the complexity
hidden in the existing implementation. Incremental replacement is slower, less
satisfying, and far more likely to succeed._

---

=== Measuring Progress

* [ ] How will you know if debt repayment is working?
* [ ] Are you tracking leading indicators (deployment frequency, change failure
rate, time to resolve) alongside the debt register?
* [ ] Can you demonstrate to the business that the investment is paying off?

_If you can't measure the impact of debt repayment, you can't justify continuing it.
Choose metrics that the business cares about, not ones that only engineers understand._

== A Final Thought

The teams that manage technical debt well share a common characteristic:
they talk about it openly, often, and without shame.

Debt isn't a sign of failure. It's a sign that the system has been through
enough real-world use to accumulate compromises. The failure is not in having
debt — it's in pretending it doesn't exist, or in treating it as someone
else's problem.

The best time to address technical debt was when it was incurred.
The second best time is now — starting with the piece that's costing you
the most.
