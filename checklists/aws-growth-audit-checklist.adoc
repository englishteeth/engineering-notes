== Foreword

The mistakes in this checklist are rarely the result of bad engineering.
They're the result of good engineers focused on their part of the system,
at a point in time, with the information they had.

The problem is that distributed systems are not the sum of their parts —
they're the product of how those parts interact, degrade, and evolve together
over time.

Most of the questions that follow don't have a service-level answer. They
require someone to step back and own the whole picture. If nobody in your
team does that, this checklist will feel uncomfortable.

That discomfort is the point.

---

== Section 1: Cost & Billing

=== Serverless Invocation Growth

* [ ] Are your Lambda invocation counts and durations being tracked against cost thresholds?
* [ ] Have you evaluated whether high-frequency, low-complexity functions that made sense at
low volume are now candidates for migration to containerised or reserved compute?

_Pay-as-you-go serverless feels like a win when you're starting out. As traffic grows,
the cost and latency profile can quietly become a problem that a different architecture
would solve more cheaply._

---

=== Data Ingress/Egress Cost Creep

* [ ] Are you actively monitoring data transfer costs — cross-region, cross-AZ, and out-to-internet?
* [ ] Do you understand which architectural decisions (where services live relative to each other)
are driving your transfer costs?

_These costs are invisible until they're not. Early placement decisions compound silently
as data volumes grow._

---

=== Idle Resource Management

* [ ] Are non-production environments scheduled to shut down outside working hours?
* [ ] Are dev and staging RDS instances, NAT Gateways, and EC2 instances running 24/7?

_If development environments are running when nobody is using them, the same pattern
almost certainly exists in production. Leaving the lights on in dev is a cultural signal,
not just a cost issue._

---

== Section 2: Architecture & Scaling

=== Re-evaluate Pay-As-You-Go vs Reserved Capacity

* [ ] Have you reviewed your workload patterns recently to assess whether they are now
predictable enough to benefit from reserved instances or Savings Plans?
* [ ] Are you paying a flexibility premium for dynamic scaling that your traffic patterns
no longer require?

_Dynamic scaling is ideal for unpredictable early-stage workloads. As traffic matures,
you may be paying significantly for flexibility you no longer need. This is a sign of
success — treat it like one._

---

=== Pico-Service Fragmentation

* [ ] Have your microservices been decomposed around domain bounded contexts, or at the
function level?
* [ ] Is each service owning a coherent domain capability, or has it been split into
units too small to be independently meaningful?

_Overly granular services increase operational overhead, network latency, and deployment
complexity without a corresponding architectural benefit. Decompose around domains,
not functions._

---

=== Shared Resource Coupling

* [ ] Are your services truly independent, or are they sharing databases, queues, or
other infrastructure?
* [ ] Can each service be deployed, scaled, and failed independently?

_Shared resources create hidden coupling that undermines independent deployment and
scaling — one of the core promises of a microservices architecture. If two services
share a database, they are not two services._

---

=== Region-Bound Feature Availability

* [ ] Have you verified that your chosen AWS region still supports all services and
capabilities your architecture depends on?
* [ ] Are you working around regional gaps you've quietly accepted?

_Not all AWS services are available in all regions. Constraints accepted early can
become embedded assumptions that are expensive to unpick later._

---

=== Data Sovereignty Drift

* [ ] As your customer base has grown — particularly internationally — are you confident
data residency and sovereignty requirements are still being met?
* [ ] Have early region choices made for latency or cost reasons created compliance
exposure as the business has scaled?

---

=== Cross-Region Cost Creep

* [ ] Are you actively tracking the cost of data replication and transfer across regions?
* [ ] Do you understand which resilience patterns are driving your cross-region spend?

_What begins as a sensible resilience pattern can become a significant and poorly
understood cost centre as data volumes grow._

---

=== The Failover Fallacy

* [ ] Has your multi-region failover strategy been tested end-to-end under realistic conditions?
* [ ] Do you know your actual RTO — not the figure in your documentation, but the time
it took the last time you practiced?

_An untested failover plan is not a failover plan. If you haven't practiced the recovery,
you don't know your real RTO — and neither does your team._

---

== Section 3: CI/CD & Deployment

=== Infrastructure as Code Discipline

* [ ] Is your IaC versioned, peer-reviewed, and subject to the same quality standards
as your application code?
* [ ] Does your IaC live inside your engineering culture, or alongside it?

_Infrastructure definitions that aren't treated with the same rigour as application code
are a liability. The system your IaC provisions is only as reliable as the discipline
behind it._

---

=== Pipeline Configuration Drift

* [ ] Are your delivery configurations actively maintained and regularly reviewed?
* [ ] Have your pipelines accumulated undocumented changes and workarounds that nobody
fully understands?
* [ ] Are your pipelines regularly exercised, or only run when a deployment is needed?

_Deployment pipelines that aren't maintained become brittle and unpredictable exactly
when you need them most — during an incident or a major release._

---

=== Monolithic Pipeline Architecture

* [ ] Is your deployment pipeline modular and composable, or a single tightly coupled process?
* [ ] Can pipeline stages be executed and tested in isolation?
* [ ] Are pipeline components reusable across services?

_A pipeline that can't be partially executed or reused will become a bottleneck as your
team and product grow._

---

=== Pipeline Naming Coherence

* [ ] Do your build pipeline components, stages, and artefacts use naming conventions
consistent with your current service and domain language?
* [ ] Does the language of your pipeline reflect the language of your codebase?

_Misalignment between project naming and pipeline naming is a quiet indicator that the
pipeline has become a separate concern from the codebase — owned by nobody, understood
by few, and increasingly risky to change._

---

== Section 4: Security & Compliance

=== Tutorial-Origin Permission Creep

* [ ] Were your initial IAM roles and security groups configured during a proof-of-concept
or tutorial phase?
* [ ] Have default and example configurations been deliberately tightened since initial build?

_AWS defaults and tutorials are deliberately open for ease of learning. Policies that
were never tightened after the initial build represent silent, compounding exposure as
the system grows around them._

---

=== IAM Naming and Intent Coherence

* [ ] Do your IAM roles, policies, and security groups have names that clearly describe
their purpose and the specific component they serve?
* [ ] Can you identify the owner and intent of each policy from its name alone?

_Vague or generic naming (`dev-role`, `test-sg`, `lambda-policy`) is a reliable indicator
that permissions were created without a clear ownership model and are unlikely to have
been reviewed since._

---

=== Permission-to-Process Traceability

* [ ] Can you trace each significant IAM permission back to a specific business or
technical requirement?
* [ ] Do you have a practice for reviewing and removing permissions that are no longer needed?

_AWS provides no native framework for permission traceability. Without deliberate practice,
permissions accumulate without context and become almost impossible to safely audit or remove._

---

=== Trust Boundary Sprawl

* [ ] As your architecture has grown and more services have been integrated, have your
trust boundaries been explicitly redrawn?
* [ ] Are your policy enforcement points adding latency that user experience or API
consumers can't absorb?

_Security that degrades user experience tends to get quietly worked around. Boundaries
that were clearly defined early can become stretched and implicit over time — enforced
in theory, bypassed in practice._

---

=== GDPR at Architectural Scale

* [ ] Was your data privacy model designed when the system was simple?
* [ ] As services have multiplied, have you re-verified where personal data lives,
how it moves, and who can access it?
* [ ] Is GDPR compliance reviewed as part of architectural change, or treated as a
one-time implementation?

_GDPR compliance isn't a checkbox — it needs to be re-verified as the architecture evolves._

---

== Section 5: Observability & Operational Maturity

=== Intentional Log Levels

* [ ] Are log levels used with discipline across your services, or has everything drifted
to DEBUG in production?
* [ ] Do your log levels reflect genuine severity and operational relevance?

_Noisy logs are as dangerous as no logs. They bury the signal you need during an incident._

---

=== Context-Rich Logging

* [ ] Do your logs describe what the system is _doing_, or simply mark code entry and exit points?
* [ ] Would an engineer unfamiliar with the codebase understand what business operation
was in progress, what the relevant state was, and what outcome occurred?

_Entry and exit markers are a development scaffold, not an operational tool. A log entry
should tell a story, not a stack trace._

---

=== Distributed Tracing

* [ ] Are you using OpenTelemetry or equivalent to trace requests across service boundaries?
* [ ] Can you correlate a user-facing issue to a specific chain of service interactions?

_In a distributed system, correlated tracing isn't optional — it's the difference between
diagnosing an incident in minutes or hours. As your service count grows, the absence of
this becomes exponentially more painful._

---

=== Alerting vs Absence of Incidents

* [ ] Is your system healthy, or have you just not had any reported incidents recently?
* [ ] Do you have proactive alerting tied to meaningful thresholds, or are you dependent
on users reporting problems?

_These are not the same thing. Without proactive alerting, your users are your monitoring._

---

=== Operational Visibility

* [ ] Do you have dashboards showing real-time throughput, error rates, and latency
across your key services?
* [ ] Can your on-call engineer assess system health in under thirty seconds?

_Operational health shouldn't require log diving to assess. If it does, your observability
isn't mature enough for your scale._

---

=== Chaos Readiness

* [ ] What happens if you kill a critical service right now?
* [ ] Is your failure response practiced and documented, or theoretical?
* [ ] When did you last simulate a significant failure?

_The question isn't whether something will fail — it's whether you'll be ready when it does.
Untested failure modes multiply as systems grow._

---

== A Final Word on Ownership

Every section in this checklist can be addressed with the right tool, practice, or process.
But tools don't fix the root cause.

The teams that outgrow their AWS setup without noticing almost always share one characteristic:
nobody owns the whole picture. Individual services are healthy. Individual teams are performing.
But the system — the interactions, the dependencies, the accumulated decisions — belongs to nobody.

A distributed system is not the sum of its parts. It is the product of how those parts
interact, degrade, and evolve together over time.

The most important question this checklist can prompt isn't technical.

*Who in your organisation is looking at the whole thing?*
