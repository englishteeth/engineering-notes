== Foreword

Microservices are easy to start and hard to get right.

The pattern promises independent deployment, team autonomy, and clean boundaries.
What it actually delivers depends almost entirely on how carefully you drew those
boundaries — and whether you've revisited them since.

Most teams I review have adopted the labels of microservices without achieving the
properties. They have many small things, deployed separately, that cannot function
independently. That's not a microservice architecture. That's a distributed monolith
with better marketing.

This checklist won't tell you whether microservices are right for you.
It will help you see whether the ones you have are doing what you thought they would.

== Section 1: Service Boundaries

=== Domain Alignment

* [ ] Can you describe each service's responsibility in a single sentence using
business domain language?
* [ ] Were your service boundaries drawn around domain capabilities, or around
technical layers (API, persistence, processing)?

_A service that is a thin wrapper around a database table is not a microservice.
It's a CRUD API with infrastructure overhead. Services should own coherent
slices of business capability._

---

=== Boundary Stability

* [ ] When a business requirement changes, how many services need to be modified?
* [ ] Do feature requests routinely span three or more services?

_If a single feature consistently requires coordinated changes across multiple
services, your boundaries are in the wrong place. Good boundaries absorb
change — they don't distribute it._

---

=== Data Ownership

* [ ] Does each service own its data exclusively?
* [ ] Are any services reading from or writing to another service's database?
* [ ] Do you have shared database schemas between services?

_If two services share a database, they are one service with two deployment
pipelines. Shared data stores create invisible coupling that makes independent
deployment a fiction._

---

=== API Contract Discipline

* [ ] Are your service APIs versioned?
* [ ] Do you have a strategy for deprecating and retiring old API versions?
* [ ] Can a service update its internal implementation without changing its API?

_The API is the boundary. If internal changes routinely force API changes,
the abstraction isn't working._

---

== Section 2: Independence & Deployment

=== Independent Deployability

* [ ] Can each service be deployed to production without coordinating with other teams?
* [ ] When was the last time you deployed a single service in isolation?
* [ ] Do you have services that must be deployed together — even informally?

_Independent deployment is the core benefit of the microservice pattern.
If you can't do it, you're paying the operational cost of distribution
without receiving the architectural benefit._

---

=== Deployment Frequency

* [ ] How often does each service get deployed independently?
* [ ] Are some services deployed frequently while others haven't been touched in months?
* [ ] Do "dormant" services receive security patches and dependency updates?

_A service that hasn't been deployed in six months is a liability.
Its deployment pipeline is untested, its dependencies are stale, and
the team's confidence in changing it is low._

---

=== Coordinated Release Ceremonies

* [ ] Do you have a release train, deployment window, or cross-service release
coordination process?
* [ ] Is there a person whose job it is to orchestrate releases across services?

_If your microservice architecture requires a release coordinator, you've
recreated the monolith's deployment coupling while adding network overhead.
The architecture isn't buying you what you think it is._

== Section 3: Operational Reality

=== Failure Isolation

* [ ] When a single service fails, what else breaks?
* [ ] Do you have circuit breakers or bulkheads between services?
* [ ] Have you tested what happens when a downstream dependency is slow
(not down — slow)?

_Distributed systems have a failure mode that monoliths don't: cascading
slowness. A slow dependency is often worse than a failed one, because it
ties up resources throughout the call chain._

---

=== Timeout and Retry Discipline

* [ ] Are timeouts explicitly configured on every inter-service call?
* [ ] Are retry policies deliberate, or are you relying on client library defaults?
* [ ] Have you checked whether retries in one layer compound with retries in another?

_Compounding retries across layers can turn a brief downstream hiccup into
a sustained load spike that takes down the entire system. This is not a
theoretical risk — it's one of the most common failure patterns in distributed
architectures._

---

=== Observability Across Boundaries

* [ ] Can you trace a single user request across all the services it touches?
* [ ] Do your logs and metrics share a common correlation ID?
* [ ] Can you diagnose a cross-service issue without logging into multiple systems?

_If you can't follow a request through your system, you're operating blind.
Distributed tracing is not a nice-to-have — it's the operational price of
admission for microservices._

---

=== Service Discovery and Routing

* [ ] Do your services find each other through a discovery mechanism, or through
hardcoded configuration?
* [ ] What happens when a service instance is retired or replaced?

_Hardcoded service locations work until they don't. And they tend to stop
working at the least convenient moment._

== Section 4: Team & Organisational Signals

=== Team-to-Service Alignment

* [ ] Does each service have a clearly identified owning team?
* [ ] Are there services that sit between teams with no clear owner?
* [ ] Can a single team make changes to their services without waiting on another team?

_Conway's Law is not a suggestion. If your team structure doesn't match your
service structure, one of them will bend — and it's usually the architecture.
Unowned services accumulate debt faster than anything else in the system._

---

=== Cognitive Load

* [ ] How many services does each team own and operate?
* [ ] Can a developer on the team explain what each of their services does and
how they interact?
* [ ] Is the team spending more time on operational toil than on feature delivery?

_There's a limit to how many services a team can own before quality degrades.
If developers can't hold the system in their head, they can't reason about
it — and unreasonable systems produce unreasonable bugs._

---

=== Shared Library Coupling

* [ ] Do your services share common libraries for business logic (not just utilities)?
* [ ] When a shared library is updated, how many services need to be rebuilt and redeployed?
* [ ] Has a shared library update ever caused a production incident?

_Shared libraries are the polite version of a shared database. They create
coupling that's invisible until a change in one library forces a cascade
of deployments across the system._

== Section 5: Data & Consistency

=== Eventual Consistency Understanding

* [ ] Does your team understand where the system is eventually consistent
versus strongly consistent?
* [ ] Have you documented the consistency guarantees your users actually experience?
* [ ] Have you had incidents caused by stale reads or out-of-order events?

_Eventual consistency isn't a problem — it's a tradeoff. But it becomes a problem
when the team doesn't know where it applies, or when the business expects consistency
the architecture can't guarantee._

---

=== Event-Driven Complexity

* [ ] If you use asynchronous messaging, can you trace the full lifecycle of a message
from publication to final consumption?
* [ ] Do you have dead letter queues, and are they monitored?
* [ ] When was the last time you reviewed what's in your dead letter queues?

_Dead letter queues are where your system quietly puts the problems it can't solve.
If nobody is looking at them, you have a growing backlog of unprocessed failures
that are invisible to your monitoring._

---

=== Cross-Service Reporting

* [ ] How do you answer questions that require data from multiple services?
* [ ] Do you have a separate read model, data warehouse, or reporting service?
* [ ] Are teams building ad-hoc queries directly against other services' databases?

_The moment someone needs data from three services to answer a business question,
the temptation to bypass service boundaries becomes overwhelming. If you don't
provide a legitimate path for cross-service reporting, people will create an
illegitimate one._

== A Final Thought

The best microservice architectures I've reviewed have a quality that's hard to quantify:
they feel calm. Each service does one thing, owns its data, deploys independently,
and fails gracefully. The teams that own them can explain what they do without
reaching for a whiteboard.

The worst ones feel frantic. Changes ripple unpredictably. Deployments require
ceremonies. Incidents are archaeological expeditions. Teams spend more time
coordinating than building.

If your architecture feels more like the second description than the first,
the answer is rarely more services. It's usually better boundaries on the
ones you have.
